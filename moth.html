Peppered Moth

Urbanization is the process by which natural landscapes are turned into cities, roads, buildings and other human made infrastructure. As human populations grow urban areas expand creating ecological challenges such as replacing natural habitats, increasing chemical, noise and light pollution, disrupting temperature patterns and altering ecosystems (Khan Academy, n.d.)





Environmental conditions act as selection pressures, favoring traits that increase survival and reproductive success. Evolution is particularly important in the context of human caused environmental change as pollution, climate change and urban expansion rapidly alter ecosystems exposing species to new selection pressures. An organism's ability to adapt and quickly adjust demonstrates that evolution is a process that shapes all species' chances of survival in changing environments (YourGenome, n.d).











Peppered Moths
The Biston betularia also known as peppered moth is one of the most famous examples of evolution by natural selection Before the Industrial Revolution, most peppered moths in England had light colored wings with dark specks thus its name, this allowed them to blend in with tree bark covered in pale lichen which is a combination of fungus and algae (What Is a Lichen?, n.d.). A genetic mutation in the moths produced dark colored wings, but these ones were much more rare because they were more visible to predators. However during the Industrial Revolution, factories began to release large amounts of soot, a fine dark powder made up of carbon, darkening tree trunks and killing lichens (Soot, 2022). As a result, dark colored moths became better camouflaged and were less likely to be eaten by birds, The dark winged colorations became an advantage that increased the moths survival in polluted urban areas.


Because dark moths survived longer and reproduced more, the genes responsible for dark coloration were passed on to more offspring. Each generation had a higher percentage of dark moths than the last one so by the late 19th century dark colored moths made up the majority of populations in heavily industrialized regions.


Later on when pollution levels decreased due to environmental regulations and cleaner air, light colored moths became more common because they regained their camouflage advantage.
